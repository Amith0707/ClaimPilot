RULE 1 — Property Damage (default)
Claims describing vehicle damage only, with no mention of injury, legal action, or fraud indicators, are categorized as Property Damage, routed to Auto Claims Team, priority Medium.

RULE 2 — Property Damage (high value)
If a Property Damage claim mentions repair estimates, total loss, or damage description exceeding a significant financial threshold, or involves multiple vehicles, priority is escalated to High even without injury.

RULE 3 — Personal Injury (precedence rule)
Any claim mentioning injury, pain, hospital, medical treatment, or bodily harm — regardless of how minor — in the context of a vehicle accident or incident, is categorized as Personal Injury and routed to Injury Specialists, taking precedence over any property damage mentioned in the same claim. IMPORTANT: injury words alone are NOT sufficient. If the message mentions injury or pain WITHOUT any accompanying vehicle, accident, crash, or collision context (e.g., just "i'm hurt" or "I'm in pain" with nothing else), there is not enough context to confirm this is an auto insurance claim — categorize as Insufficient Information with Low confidence instead, per Rule 10.

RULE 4 — Personal Injury (priority)
Personal Injury claims are priority High by default. Only downgrade to Medium if the injury is explicitly described as resolved, minor, and not requiring ongoing treatment. If the claim describes severe, critical, life-threatening, unconscious, not breathing, fatal, death, or passed away language, priority remains High and the reasoning field must explicitly flag it as top-severity requiring immediate emergency-level human follow-up, ambulance dispatch, or escalation beyond standard claims processing.

RULE 5 — Litigation trigger (legal language)
A claim is categorized as Litigation and routed to Legal Team with priority High only when the legal language indicates active or adversarial legal action against the insurer — such as "I'm suing," "my lawyer is filing a suit," "we are pursuing legal action," or "I've retained an attorney to take action against you." Incidental mentions of legal counsel — such as a lawyer merely advising the claimant on how to report or document a claim ("my lawyer said I should report this") — do NOT trigger Litigation on their own. In such incidental cases, categorize based on the substantive content of the claim (injury, damage, etc.) per the normal precedence rules, and note the incidental legal mention in the reasoning field without it driving the category.

RULE 6 — Fraud Review (mismatch signal)
Claims where the described severity contradicts other stated details (e.g., "minor damage" paired with a very high repair estimate, or inconsistent incident timelines) MUST be categorized as Fraud Review, routed to Fraud Investigation Unit, priority High — NOT Property Damage, even when the repair estimate alone would otherwise trigger Rule 2's cost escalation. A mismatch between described severity and financial/documentary evidence is a stronger signal than cost alone.
Explicit trigger check: whenever a claim uses minimizing language for damage — words like "minor," "small," "just a," "little," "slight" — AND ALSO states a repair estimate or cost that is disproportionately high (e.g., a "minor scratch" claiming a repair cost in the lakhs, or "small dent" with a five-figure-or-higher estimate), this is a mismatch by definition and Rule 6 applies. Do NOT default to Rule 2 in this situation, even though Rule 2's cost-threshold condition also technically applies — Rule 6 takes precedence whenever this specific minimizing-language-plus-high-cost pattern is present. Example: "Minor scratch on my bumper, repair estimate is 8 lakh rupees" — minimizing language ("minor scratch") + disproportionate cost (8 lakh rupees) = Fraud Review, not Property Damage.
CRITICAL — check for negation before applying this rule: minimizing words that are NEGATED or contradicted do NOT count as minimizing language. "The damage is NOT minor," "this isn't just a small dent," or any phrasing where the claimant asserts the damage is genuinely severe and consistent with the cost, means severity and cost AGREE — there is no mismatch, and Rule 6 does NOT apply in this case. Read the actual meaning of the sentence, not just the presence of trigger words like "minor" or "small." Example: "The scratch is definitely NOT minor, the 8 lakh estimate seems right for the damage" = Property Damage (high value) per Rule 2, NOT Fraud Review, because the claimant is asserting the severity matches the cost, not minimizing it.


RULE 7 — Fraud Review (explicit suspicion language)
Claims containing language suggesting staged incidents, multiple recent claims from the same party in a short timeframe, repeated or similar claims filed close together, or explicit mention of prior fraud investigations are categorized as Fraud Review, routed to Fraud Investigation Unit, priority High.

RULE 8 — Claim Status Inquiry
Claims that reference an existing/prior claim and ask about its status, delay, or progress — rather than reporting a new incident — are categorized as Claim Status Inquiry, routed to Customer Support Team. A "reference" is ANY mention of an ongoing issue, even vague or informal — examples include "my car," "nothing works," "this issue," a claim number, or ANY stated time period waited (e.g., "3 days," "weeks," "a while," "months"). Heavy emotional tone or all-caps does NOT disqualify a message from this category if any such reference is present; apply this rule BEFORE considering Rule 10.
Priority is High when at least one concrete escalation threshold is met: a stated time period of 2 or more weeks (or any phrasing implying multiple weeks, such as "weeks" plural without a number, "a month," etc.), 2 or more prior contact attempts, or phrases like "still no update," "no response," or "haven't heard back." A stated time period under 2 weeks (e.g., "3 days," "a few days") does NOT meet the threshold — priority is Medium in that case, but the claim still belongs in Claim Status Inquiry, never Insufficient Information.
Low priority never applies to Claim Status Inquiry.

RULE 9 — General Inquiry
Claims that are actual questions about coverage, policy terms, or general insurance information — with no specific incident being reported — are categorized as General Inquiry, routed to Customer Support Team, priority Low. General Inquiry requires an identifiable, answerable question or topic (e.g., "does my policy cover rental cars?"). Emotional venting, complaints without specifics, or messages with no actual question do NOT qualify as General Inquiry — see Rules 10 and 13.

RULE 10 — Insufficient Information (fallback)
Reserve this category ONLY for messages with ZERO reference to any claim, incident, vehicle, or ongoing issue — e.g., "broken," "help," "I am angry" (no company or claim mention), "i'm hurt" (no accident context), or content-free noise. If a message references ANYTHING concrete — "my car," a claim number, a length of time waited, a specific incident, a company complaint — even briefly and even with strong emotional tone, it has enough substance to belong to another category (most often Rule 8, Claim Status Inquiry) and must NOT be treated as Insufficient Information.
Priority is Low, confidence Low, for genuine Insufficient Information cases.
Team assignment splits by whether a human could act on the message as-is:
(a) Zero-reference messages (no claim, incident, vehicle, or issue mentioned at all) — route to Automated Response. A human reviewer could not determine intent from these either; the correct action is an automated reply requesting details (what happened, when, vehicle involved, any injuries), which does not require human time.
(b) Content-rich but genuinely unclassifiable messages — the message describes a real, specific situation with meaningful detail, but it conflicts with itself, spans categories in ways the precedence rules don't resolve, or describes something unusual that fits no category — route to Manual Review Team. These are the ONLY Insufficient Information cases that should consume human review time.
Test: "Could a human reviewer do anything with this message other than ask for more details?" If no → Automated Response. If yes → Manual Review Team. Note: a message with ANY concrete reference should first be checked against Rules 1-9 before falling back here at all — do not default to Insufficient Information just because tone is intense or the message is short if a concrete reference is present.

RULE 11 — Ambiguous claims (tie-break rule)
When a claim reasonably fits more than one category — most commonly Personal Injury vs. Property Damage — the reasoning field must explicitly state which signal was prioritized and why (default: injury takes precedence per Rule 3, provided vehicle/accident context is present).

RULE 12 — Multiple signals present
If a claim contains signals for more than one category (e.g., injury + legal language), prioritize in this order: Litigation > Personal Injury > Fraud Review > Property Damage > Claim Status Inquiry > General Inquiry. State the overriding signal in the reasoning field. This precedence order applies only when each signal genuinely meets its own rule's trigger conditions (e.g., legal language must be adversarial per Rule 5 to count as a Litigation signal).

RULE 13 — Angry/emotional tone
Anger, frustration, or emotional language alone — without any accompanying mention of legal action, attorney, lawsuit, fraud, staged incidents, injury, or specific damage details — does NOT indicate Litigation, Fraud Review, or Personal Injury, and does NOT raise priority on its own. Handle by content, not tone: (a) if frustration accompanies a reference to an existing claim or unresolved issue (e.g., "my issue is not resolved," a claim number), treat as Claim Status Inquiry per Rule 8, with priority determined strictly by Rule 8's concrete thresholds; (b) if the message is ONLY emotional content with no claim reference, no question, and no substantive information whatsoever (e.g., "I am angry"), categorize as Insufficient Information per Rule 10 — NOT General Inquiry; (c) never route to a category just because tone is intense.

RULE 14 — Missing required documentation
If a claim references injury or significant damage but does not mention any supporting documentation (police report, medical record, photos), note this in the reasoning field as a flag for manual follow-up, without downgrading confidence unless the description itself is too vague to categorize.

RULE 15 — Off-topic / non-claim input
Messages unrelated to an insurance claim, accident, policy question, or claim status — such as general conversation, unrelated questions, greetings, or test input with no insurance-relevant content — should be categorized as "Out of Scope", NOT "Insufficient Information". Route to "Automated Response", NOT "Manual Review Team", since these messages do not require human claims-handling attention and should not consume reviewer time. Priority Low, confidence Low, with reasoning noting the message appears unrelated to an insurance claim. Distinction from Rule 10: use Out of Scope when the message clearly has nothing to do with insurance at all; use Insufficient Information when the message might plausibly relate to a claim but lacks the detail to tell.
